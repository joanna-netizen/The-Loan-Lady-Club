Important: How the Seller Credit Works

The 2% Seller Credit Rule

Because this is an investment property, the maximum seller credit a lender will allow is 2% of the purchase price. On Option A, that is $4,000. On Option B, that is $4,600. These credits go toward your closing costs at the table.

The seller credit is not guaranteed. It has to be negotiated as part of your purchase offer. If the seller does not agree to it, or agrees to a lower amount, the difference comes out of your own funds at closing. Your cash to close figures above assume the full 2% is received.

If the seller does not provide the full credit, you would need to cover that gap from your own documented assets. This is something to keep in mind as you and Magda structure your offer.

Important: Reserve Requirement

What the 6-Month Reserve Means

Because this is an investment property, the lender requires you to show six months of your full monthly payment sitting in a documented account after closing. That means your mortgage, taxes, insurance, and PMI combined. The money must still be there once the down payment and closing costs have been paid.

On Option A, that amount is approximately $8,909.70. On Option B, it is approximately $10,156.14. These funds do not get spent at closing. They simply need to exist in your account and be verifiable through your bank statements.

A 401k or retirement account can satisfy this requirement as long as the funds are accessible, either through a hardship withdrawal or a loan provision. If you plan to use retirement funds for reserves, let me know and we will document that correctly.

When you look at your total cash picture, you need enough to cover the down payment, the closing costs (net of seller credit), and the reserves all at the same time. We will verify that together once your bank statements are in.
