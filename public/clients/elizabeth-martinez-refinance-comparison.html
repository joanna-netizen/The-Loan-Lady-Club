Important Context

The Escrow Situation

When your loan was set up, your lender collected less each month than was actually needed to cover your property taxes and homeowner's insurance. Over time, that gap added up. Your escrow account is now empty, and your property taxes are due within the next 60 days.

This is not uncommon, and it is not a penalty situation. But it does mean that part of what a refinance can accomplish for you right now is solving that problem cleanly at closing. Both options below include a full replenishment of your escrow account so you are not sitting exposed going into tax season.

Both options also allow you to skip your April mortgage payment, which is a standard benefit of refinancing. The interest for that period folds into the new loan rather than being forgiven outright, but it does free up your April cash flow while the new loan gets established.

How to Think About This

What Each Option Is Really Doing

A
Option A is a clean reset. It lowers your payment, cuts your PMI nearly in half, fixes the escrow shortage, and gets you into a lower rate. Your new loan balance is only slightly above your current payoff because the extra amount covers closing costs and replenishes the escrow account. You come to closing with virtually nothing out of pocket, and your total monthly housing cost goes from $3,000.49 down to $2,657.02. That is a difference of $343.47 every single month.
B
Option B is designed for a household that has an immediate cash need. The loan amount is higher, which is what generates the cash back at closing. The fees worksheet shows an estimated cash figure of $4,186.58, but the appraisal and credit report fees are paid upfront before closing, which brings the actual amount you receive back down to approximately $3,400. The trade-off is a higher monthly payment compared to Option A and a PMI figure that stays closer to what you are paying today. You still save $257.45 per month against your current loan, but the ongoing monthly benefit is smaller because the loan is carrying more weight.
1
The rate is 6.125% on both options. The difference is entirely about loan size and what you walk away with at closing versus what your ongoing monthly obligation looks like for the life of the loan.
2
On Option A, you skip April and your existing escrow balance will be returned to you by your current servicer approximately 30 days after closing. Those two things together can create meaningful short-term cash flow relief even without a cash-out component.
3
On Option B, the approximately $3,400 cash back needs to be weighed against the higher ongoing monthly cost relative to Option A. This option makes the most sense when there is a specific use for that cash that provides more value to your household than the additional monthly savings you would receive under Option A.
"Neither option is the wrong answer. The right one depends on what your household needs most right now. That is what we will figure out together."
